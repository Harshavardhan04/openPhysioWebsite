Project Background

Our project is privileged to collaborate with Roche, a global pioneer in healthcare based in Switzerland, dedicated to transforming personal healthcare through innovation, and Great Ormond Street Hospital (GOSH), an internationally renowned institution at the forefront of pediatric care and research. This partnership brings together the finest minds in healthcare, from data scientists to physiotherapists, under the shared mission of achieving groundbreaking advancements in our project.

Leveraging Roche's cutting-edge expertise in medical research and GOSH's unparalleled experience in pediatric healthcare, our project is poised to set new standards in the industry. With the mentorship and guidance of seasoned professionals from these esteemed organizations, we are committed to navigating the complexities of healthcare challenges and delivering solutions that make a lasting difference.

This collaboration embodies our dedication to excellence, innovation, and the power of teamwork in pioneering the future of healthcare. Together, we are not just undertaking a project; we are shaping the future of personalized care for generations to come.

   PAIN POINTS   

  • Outdated Paper Methods: Physiotherapy data recording currently relies on archaic paper-based methods, which are inefficient and prone to errors.
  • Complex Data Management: The need to handle thousands of data points for diverse physiotherapy requirements presents significant challenges in data capture, integration, and visualization.
  • Lack of Standardization: The absence of a standardized approach to physiotherapy data management complicates the seamless integration of data into hospital systems.

   OpenPhysio CURES   

  • FHIR Compliance and NHS Standards: Ensuring that all data is FHIR-compliant and meets NHS standards for processing, retrieval, and storage, setting a new standard for physiotherapy data management.
  • Intuitive Interface for All Skill Levels: A React.js-based front-end interface that allows physiotherapists to manage patient data smoothly and securely, regardless of their tech skills.
  • Intuitive Data Visualization: Our platform transforms complex physiotherapy data into intuitive and engaging visual representations, enabling physiotherapists and patients to easily monitor and understand patient progress over time. This visual approach not only simplifies the tracking of therapeutic outcomes but also empowers patients to actively participate in their own recovery journey.

The overall project goal

Following client consultations, our primary objective is to develop a FHIR-compliant backend for robust data handling and an intuitive front end designed for physiotherapists to seamlessly input, visualize, and interact with patient data, irrespective of their technical proficiency.

Requirement Gathering & User Interviews

We initiated our engagement by hosting an introductory call with all stakeholders and clients to familiarize ourselves with their roles in our project. During this call, we reviewed the project brief together and captured their insights. Subsequently, we organized a second call where our team conducted two tailored interviews—one with the Roche and GOSH technical team and another with our end-users, the physiotherapists. These interviews, detailed below, were chosen for their semi-structured format, allowing us to explore in-depth and clarify any ambiguities through follow-up questions.

  • Interview 1
  • Interview 2


Q: What do you look for with patient improvement?

A: Simply put, patients have guide joint angles, for example, functional knees should be 120-140 degrees. These measurements are available online, or you can reach out to me.This will help you to access/create base values. We also compare the difference between the left and right parts, and how strong the patient is against the physiotherapist providing resistance. This is hard to quantity, but should generally remain constant if the same physiotherapist is judging it per patient

Q: With respect to data visualisation, what sort of graphs do you think would be useful?

A: Line charts would be an adequate choice, with time on the x-axis and improvement (for example, angle) on the y-axis. A horizontal red line represents the target (normal) improvement level. You can visualise how the patient is progressing and question stagnation.

Q: Do you take physical measurements?

A: Yes, for angles I measure degrees using goniometers, and for strength it is possible to use the Barthel and Borg index though these aren’t generally used down the line (at a high level). For strength, you can also measure the number of reps of a certain exercise.

Q: What is the typical process of patient treatment?

A: To start, we look for a return to mobility. Then, we look at both strength and mobility, for example, the physiotherapist resisting movement through the joint and the patient attempting to oppose that.


Q: What is FHIR?

A: It’s just a Microsoft FHIR Server - there are two kinds and we have one of them. You can refer to the Microsoft documentation to find out the differences between them. We’ll send you the details.

Q: What will the starting point of our system be from a UI perspective?

A: You can assume you already have the patient details stored on the EHR system, so you would start from the point of selecting a patient’s record, after which you can go straight to the crux of the system.

Q: So we would be using a web-based front end, connected to a Python backend using REST API, using OAuth to ensure security. Does this sound okay?

A: Yeah, that’s what ‘Smart on FHIR’ specifies, it kind of lets you wrap any reasonable web app, and there’s good documentation on how to do it with Python. If it’s difficult to mock the authentication part at your end, I would worry about that secondarily, just to the extent that you’re not doing anything that would completely break the system. Focus more on the crux of the problem, unless of course you find a great test suite somewhere and it’s very easy to set up.

Q: Is there a standard UI format that the NHS likes using in applications?

A: No, very much not so. In the NHS, most hospitals use one of three or four big EHR systems, all of which are very different. You will not be violating any holy rites of UI standards as there aren’t any



Requirement Gathering - Key Insights

Our interviews provided substantial information. We learned from physiotherapists about international standards like LOINC and SNOMED codes, which are crucial for classifying health information globally. This knowledge prompted us to consider incorporating these standards into our system to enhance its functionality and relevance. We also obtained detailed insights into various measurements and scales, which informed our decisions on designing effective visual tools for physiotherapists. Furthermore, the technical teams from Roche and GOSH were instrumental in guiding us toward key documentation and resources about FHIR, offering clear direction for our project's initiation. Their assistance was vital in setting up a strong foundation for our work.

MoSCoW List

After completing the research on our project's requirements, taking into account a wide array of scenarios, including outlier cases, we convened with our client to present a finalised MoSCoW list.

Must Have
  • The user should be able to add , delete and name new fields
  • Need to be able to update existing data
  • The user should be able input desired values
  • The user should be able to input qualitative data, in a textbox in our application
  • The user should be able to see past data of the a singular session from the past in a file
  • Generate graphs using the data that the user has inputted and the past data
  • Able to connect to the with broader systems and be compatible with existing backend infrastructure
Should Have
  • The user should be able to see past data in a csv format (the user should be able to download a csv file)
  • The user should be able to see past doctor notes in a csv format (the user should be able to download a csv file)
Could Have
  • Creating a balloon popping game in Unity to show how data is taken in and displayed in our system
  • Multi-language
  • Observe frequency of data and data storage overheads as generated reports
  • Take camera input to record data values for particlar snomeds by extracting motion angles (e.g. rotation angle of arm). To be used for demonstration purpose.
Won't Have
  • We cannot record all parts of the body when recording angle measurements - especially for the game (due to inability to involve all body parts)



Throughout the development process, some requirements underwent minor adjustments and shifts in priority, yet the majority remained the same. This has acted as a beacon throughout the entire development of our project.
